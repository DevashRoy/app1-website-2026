Energy Inequality

Renewable energy is growing around the world, but access to clean and affordable energy is still unequal. Low-income communities and developing countries are often left behind, facing higher pollution, higher energy costs, and fewer opportunities to benefit from renewable technologies.

Explore the Issue

What Is Energy Inequality?

Energy inequality happens when some groups of people have less access to reliable, affordable, and clean energy than others. Wealthier communities often have better infrastructure, lower exposure to pollution, and greater access to technologies like solar panels and electric vehicles. Meanwhile, marginalized communities may experience power outages, higher utility costs, and greater health risks caused by fossil fuel pollution.

Environmental & Social Impacts

Health Risks

Communities near power plants and industrial zones often experience higher asthma, respiratory illness, and heart disease rates due to pollution.

Economic Burden

Families with lower incomes often spend more of their paycheck on electricity and heating, making it harder to afford other necessities.

Climate Vulnerability

Extreme heat waves and storms affect vulnerable neighborhoods more severely due to weak infrastructure and limited resources.

Limited Clean Energy Access

Solar panels, EVs, and home batteries can be expensive upfront, preventing many communities from benefiting from renewable energy.

Possible Solutions

Community Solar

Shared solar projects allow multiple households to benefit from renewable energy without installing panels on their own homes.

Government Investment

Policies and funding can help expand clean energy infrastructure in underserved communities.

Energy Efficiency Programs

Weatherization, efficient appliances, and modern insulation reduce energy costs and emissions.

Energy Democracy

Giving communities control over local energy systems can improve equity and sustainability.

Global Examples

Energy inequality affects communities around the world differently. Many low-income and rural populations struggle to access affordable, clean, and reliable renewable energy.

India

Millions of people in rural India still experience unreliable electricity access. Many villages depend on coal and diesel generators, which increase pollution and health problems.

Community solar programs are helping provide cleaner and more reliable electricity while lowering long-term energy costs.

Source: World Bank Energy Program

Sub-Saharan Africa

Many communities in Sub-Saharan Africa still live without stable electricity. This limits access to healthcare, education, refrigeration, and economic opportunities.

Renewable microgrids and small solar systems are helping remote communities gain affordable and sustainable energy access.

Source: International Energy Agency (IEA)

United States

In the United States, low-income neighborhoods and communities of color often pay higher percentages of income on electricity.

These communities are also more likely to live near fossil fuel infrastructure, leading to increased pollution and health risks.

Community solar programs and government assistance projects are helping improve renewable energy access.

Source: U.S. Department of Energy

Latin America

Some rural communities in Latin America struggle with limited access to electricity due to poverty and isolated geography.

Renewable energy projects, especially solar and hydroelectric systems, are helping improve energy access in remote areas.

However, some projects have also raised concerns about land rights and environmental justice for Indigenous communities.

Source: United Nations Sustainable Energy Reports
